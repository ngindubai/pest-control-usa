Compare the seasons: year-round Blackland Prairie fire ants vs. year-round termites

Taylor's Blackland Prairie geology creates the same year-round pest dynamic that Ennis sees to the northeast: clay soils that retain moisture support both fire ant colony density and subterranean termite activity at above-average levels for Central Texas. Fire ant pressure in Taylor yards is most visible in the warm months when new mounds appear after rain events, but colonies are active underground throughout the year. Termite colonies never enter a dormant phase in Williamson County's climate: spring swarms from February through April are the public sign of colonies that have been active year-round. Both pests require annual management programs, not one-time seasonal treatments.

The contrast that matters: eastern Blackland Prairie vs. western limestone Taylor pest geography

Like Bastrop, Taylor has a subtle east-west pest divide driven by its position at the edge of the Blackland Prairie's transition to rockier Central Texas terrain. The eastern and central residential areas of Taylor sit on clay-heavy Blackland soils where fire ants and termites dominate. The western edges of Williamson County transition toward rocky limestone terrain where scorpions are more common than in the pure prairie areas. Homeowners on Taylor's western growth edges, near the rockier terrain, should add scorpion exclusion to their fire ant and termite program. Those in the central and eastern Blackland Prairie zones focus primarily on termite monitoring and fire ant management.

Taylor questions, side by side

Why do fire ants come back so quickly in Taylor yards?

Williamson County's Blackland clay soils retain moisture well, which supports large fire ant colony chambers that survive through dry periods and rebuild quickly after treatment. Individual mound treatment temporarily eliminates visible mounds but does not affect the broader colony system. Broadcast bait in spring and fall reaches queens throughout the colony network and provides more sustained suppression.

Are termites more active in Taylor because of the clay soils?

Yes. Clay soils retain the moisture that subterranean termites need for colony activity and movement through the soil. Williamson County's clay-rich Blackland Prairie soils support larger and more consistently active termite colonies than the sandier or rockier soils found in other parts of Central Texas. Annual inspection is the standard care approach for Taylor homeowners.

Are scorpions common in Taylor?

In the central and eastern areas of Taylor on Blackland Prairie clay, scorpions are uncommon. On the western edges of Taylor and Williamson County where the terrain transitions toward rocky limestone features, striped bark scorpions are more regularly encountered. Knowing which soil type your property sits on is the key factor for determining whether scorpion exclusion should be part of your pest plan.

How does Taylor's rapid growth affect pest pressure?

Construction activity on Blackland Prairie land disturbs established fire ant colonies temporarily and then re-concentrates them at the edges of the new development. New homeowners in recently built Taylor subdivisions often see high fire ant mound density in their first year or two as displaced populations re-establish in new lots. This is expected and responds to the same broadcast bait management used for established properties.
