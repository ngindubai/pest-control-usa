Do not waste effort here

The brief is explicit that Google's own documentation (5 June update) lists tactics that do not help AI search, so we should not spend a single hour on them: no llms.txt file, no AI specific schema beyond standard structured data, no special content chunking for AI, and no paid brand mentions on low quality domains. Good news: this repo currently contains none of these, so there is nothing to remove. The correct path to AI Overview citation is boring and is exactly what the fixes below deliver: rank organically, be discoverable, answer the question directly, and mark up entities cleanly.

Critical findings fix these first, in order

C1. The 2,150 city pages are missing from the sitemap that actually ships

CRITICALSONNET OK
What
There are two sitemap generators fighting each other. The native Next.js one at src/app/sitemap.ts is the one that lands in the deployed out/ folder, and it lists only the home page, the service pages, and the 51 state pages. It never loops over the cities. The second generator, next-sitemap, is configured well and would include the cities, but it runs after the static export has already finished and writes its files into public/, which is git ignored and never copied into out/. So its output never reaches the live site.
Why it matters
A sitemap is the primary way Google discovers thousands of deep pages quickly. With the city pages absent from it, and given they are also unlinked (see C2), Google has almost no route to find them. This is the single most likely mechanical reason for weak indexation of the money pages, and it maps directly to the brief's worry about "US local organic traffic" and the "thin content" open loop. Many of these pages may not be thin at all, they may simply be invisible.
The fix
Add the cities to src/app/sitemap.ts by mapping over the cities array, and retire the dead next-sitemap path so there is one source of truth. At roughly 2,200 URLs total the single file is still well under the 50,000 URL limit, so no split index is needed yet.
Trade-off
Almost none. The only minor decision is whether to keep next-sitemap for its per type priority values or fold those into the native file. Folding them in is simpler and removes a moving part, which is the recommendation. Removing next-sitemap also means the robots.txt it was generating must be replaced by a native src/app/robots.ts (this repo already has one, so confirm it is the survivor).
Files: src/app/sitemap.ts:94, next-sitemap.config.js, .gitignore:21, DEPLOY.md:9

C2. State pages do not link to their own city pages (orphan risk)

CRITICALSONNET OK
What
On a state page the "Cities We Serve" grid renders each city as a plain <div> with a pin icon and a name, not as a link (src/app/locations/[slug]/page.tsx:333). Worse, it reads from a hardcoded list of only five city name strings in locations.ts, not from the real city data. The helper that returns a state's actual cities, getCitiesByState(), exists but is never called. So a state page offers zero inbound links to any of its city pages. The header and footer link only to states and services. The only inbound links any city page receives are the "nearby cities" links on sibling city pages.
Why it matters
Internal links are how Google distributes crawl priority and ranking signal, and how it reaches deep pages. A page with no internal links pointing to it is an orphan. Combined with C1 (no sitemap entry), a city that no other city happens to list as "nearby" is effectively undiscoverable. This is the second half of the same discoverability failure and must be fixed alongside C1.
The fix
Change the state page to call getCitiesByState(state.slug) and render each city as a real <Link> to /locations/{stateSlug}/{citySlug}/. This single change gives every city page a strong, topical inbound link from its parent, and turns the state pages into genuine hub pages.
Trade-off
A few large states will now list a long column of city links. That is fine for SEO and normal for local directories, but for user experience consider grouping by county or showing the top cities first with the rest under a "view all" region. That polish is optional and should not delay the link fix itself.
Files: src/app/locations/[slug]/page.tsx:333, src/data/cities/index.ts:120

High priority real risk or high value, do soon

H1. Fabricated review ratings are baked into schema on every page

HIGHDECISION FOR GARETH
What
A star rating and review count are hardcoded in three places that do not agree: site.ts says 4.9 and "12,400+", the footer's business schema says 12400, and the reviews page says 12847 with an invented star breakdown that adds up to that number. The footer sits in the root layout, so its aggregateRating is attached to the business entity on every single page of the site, with no real, verifiable reviews backing it up.
Why it matters
This is exactly the pattern Google's review snippet and spam policies restrict: self serving star ratings in structured data that are not based on genuine, collectible reviews. The 29 June brief's lead story is the June 2026 spam update and an unconfirmed movement that "hit spam tactics specifically," with this site flagged as highest exposure. Fabricated rating markup is a textbook manual action and rich result penalty trigger. It is the most reputationally risky item in the codebase.
The fix
Two honest options. (a) Remove all aggregateRating and Review schema until the site has a real, auditable review source, and present testimonials as plain page content without rating markup. (b) If the ratings are backed by real reviews the business can produce on request, wire them to a single source of truth so the three numbers match, and only mark up reviews that genuinely exist. Option (a) is the safe default and the recommendation unless Gareth confirms the reviews are real.
Trade-off
Removing the star markup means losing the review stars that can appear in search results, which do lift click through. That is a genuine short term cost. But keeping fabricated markup risks a penalty that would cost far more, and it is a policy violation regardless of the click benefit. This is a business and compliance decision, not a purely technical one, which is why it is tagged for Gareth rather than auto executed.
Files: src/config/site.ts:35, src/components/layout/Footer.tsx:135, src/app/reviews/page.tsx:161

H2. Some "nearby city" links point to pages that do not exist (404s)

HIGHSONNET OK
What
The nearby cities component always builds its link using the current city's state slug (parts.tsx:69). But some nearbyCities entries point across a state line. Burlington, Vermont lists Albany and Providence as nearby, so the code produces /locations/vermont/albany/ and /locations/vermont/providence/, which do not exist and return the not found page.
Why it matters
Since these cross links are, per C2, currently the only inbound internal links city pages get, having a slice of them 404 wastes crawl budget and throws away link equity on dead ends. It also gives users a broken experience. Once C2 is fixed the reliance on these links drops, but they should still be correct.
The fix
Give each nearbyCities entry its own state slug in the data (so a link can cross state lines correctly), and have the component use that. Where a nearby city genuinely has no page yet, drop the link rather than render a dead one. A short verification script should confirm every nearby link resolves to a real generated page.
Trade-off
Adding a state slug to nearby entries touches the data type and every nearby array. It is a wide but mechanical change. The safer interim fix, if a full data migration is too large for one block, is to filter out any nearby link whose target page does not exist, which removes the 404s immediately with no schema change.
Files: src/components/templates/parts.tsx:63, data e.g. src/data/cities/vermont.ts:108

H3. Page openings describe geography instead of answering the query

HIGHOPUS ADVISED
What
Across all five templates the first body paragraph is the city's intro, which is good because it is per city. But the lead sentence is usually a scene set: "Vacaville occupies the inland Solano County valley," "Whittier sits at the base of the Puente Hills." Only about 54 of 2,151 intros literally begin with "Pest control in," and only about 165 mention the word "pest" in the first ninety characters. Templates B and C push the substance down further with a generic marketing headline and a hardcoded "Local, licensed treatment" hero block above the real copy.
Why it matters
The brief's second story is the whole reason this matters now. Google formally endorsed GEO, AI Overviews appear on 5 to 15 percent of searches, and the one thing that earns a citation is "content that directly and completely answers the query" in the opening. A page that opens with valley geography is a weaker candidate than one that opens with "Pest control in El Cajon means dealing first with Argentine ants year round, then..." One page in this repo, El Cajon, already follows the ideal pattern and should be the model.
The fix
Rewrite the first sentence of each intro to lead with a direct, specific answer to "pest control in {city}," naming the dominant local pest and its timing, then flow into the existing geography. The El Cajon intro is the template. This is the highest value content change for AI Overview capture.
Trade-off and why Opus
The danger is obvious: if 2,151 intros all get the same "Pest control in {city} means dealing with {pest}" opening, we trade a geography lead for a new near duplicate fingerprint, which is precisely the scaled content pattern the spam update targets. So this is not a find and replace. The opening must vary in structure while still front loading the answer, and every rewrite touches health adjacent pest claims that must stay factually correct (YMYL). That judgment is why Opus should design the rewrite rubric and hand check a sample batch before any bulk pass. Sonnet can execute the bulk only against that rubric and the Auditor gate. Doing this fix badly is worse than not doing it.
Files: intro copy in src/data/cities/*.ts; template heroes CityTemplateB.tsx:14, CityTemplateC.tsx:19

Medium priority clear wins, lower urgency

M1. Schema references a logo and social image that do not exist

MEDIUMSONNET OK
What and why
The footer's organization schema points at /logo.png and /og-image.jpg, but the public/ folder holds only placeholder SVGs, so both are 404. A logo is required for organization rich results, so this quietly disqualifies the site from them. There are also no Open Graph or Twitter share images anywhere, despite the layout declaring a large image card, so social and chat shares render without a preview image.
The fix
Add a real logo.png and a default og-image.jpg to public/, and set a default Open Graph image in the root metadata. Optionally generate per page OG images later.
Trade-off
Requires real brand artwork, which is a design task, not a code task. Until the assets exist the schema should not claim them, so the interim safe step is to remove the broken references so schema validates cleanly.
Files: src/components/layout/Footer.tsx:107, src/app/layout.tsx:38

M2. Breadcrumbs are shown but not marked up as schema

MEDIUMSONNET OK
What and why
Every city and state page shows a Home › Service Areas › State › City breadcrumb trail as real links, but no BreadcrumbList structured data is emitted. That is a free rich result and a clean signal of site structure to both classic search and AI systems, left on the table.
The fix
Add a BreadcrumbList node to the schema graph on city and state pages, built from the same trail the UI already renders.
Trade-off
None of note. Pure addition.

M3. No entity glue: no @id linking, no single Organization or WebSite

MEDIUMSONNET OK
What and why
Not one schema node anywhere carries an @id, so the several business nodes (the footer one on every page, the per city one, the one inside each Service) look to Google like separate, unrelated businesses that happen to share a name. There is also no top level Organization and no WebSite node. The brief singles out "entity authority and structured data" as one of only three things that actually drive AI Overview citation, so this is a direct GEO lever, not just tidiness.
The fix
Introduce one canonical Organization and one WebSite node with stable @id values, and have every other node reference them by @id. Centralize schema in a small helper so the three current copies stop drifting.
Trade-off
A modest refactor to route schema through one helper. Worth it, since it also fixes the three way review count drift and the duplicate business nodes at the same time.
Files: src/components/layout/Footer.tsx:101, src/app/**/page.tsx schema blocks, new src/lib/schema.ts

M4. Template headings repeat on nearly every page in each variant

MEDIUMOPUS ADVISED
What and why
The data driven section headings are healthy, almost all unique. But each template hardcodes a fixed set of H2s that change only the city name, for example "The pests that matter in {city}," "Keeping pests out of {city} homes," "What treatment costs here." Those repeat on roughly 100 percent of that template's pages. The project's own rule (the Optimizer soul) says no H2 should repeat on more than 30 percent of a variant's pages, so the templates violate the site's own anti duplication standard. This feeds the same scaled content fingerprint the spam update looks for.
The fix
Give each template two or three heading variants for its fixed sections and rotate them by slug hash, and where possible drive headings from the data (as Template D already does by promoting FAQ questions to H2s). The goal is that no single H2 string dominates a variant bucket.
Trade-off and why Opus
Heading rotation must stay within the anti penalty engine's rules (five templates, bucket share bands, fingerprint audit) and must read naturally, not like spun text. That is a design judgment about the diversification system, so Opus should set the heading variant sets and the rotation logic. Sonnet can then apply them mechanically. Low urgency, but it closes a real self inflicted duplication gap.
Files: src/components/templates/CityTemplateA.tsx:35 and the equivalent hardcoded H2s in B, C, D, E

M5. Canonical URLs drop the trailing slash the site actually serves

MEDIUMSONNET OK
What and why
The site is configured with trailingSlash: true, so pages live at /locations/x/y/, and all internal links use that form. But the canonical tags are written without the trailing slash. A self canonical that does not match the served URL is a small inconsistency that can muddy which version Google treats as authoritative.
The fix
Append the trailing slash to every canonical and Open Graph URL so they match the served form exactly.
Trade-off
None. One character per URL, applied consistently.

MEDIUMSONNET OK
What and why
The footer links to /blog and blog posts, but there is no blog route in the app, so those are dead links sitewide. Sitewide dead links waste crawl budget and read as a quality signal problem at scale.
The fix
Either remove the blog links until a blog exists, or build the blog route (the data types for posts already exist). Removing is the quick, safe step; building is a future content play the brief's "agent content refresh" idea could feed.
Trade-off
Removing links loses planned internal structure, but dead links are worse than absent ones. Recommend removing now, building later.

M7. Placeholder business address and price-less offers weaken local and service schema

MEDIUMSONNET OK
What and why
The site config carries a placeholder headquarters address ("1 Pest Control Plaza, Dallas") used in the sitewide business schema while the business claims nationwide service, which is an odd and slightly risky signal. Service schema emits an Offer with a "Starting from" description but no actual price or currency, so it is not a valid priced offer. The city business node also bundles the state into the city name ("Fairbanks, AK" as a City), which is malformed.
The fix
Decide the real NAP story: if there is no single storefront, model the business as an Organization with a service area rather than a single address LocalBusiness. Give offers a real price range or drop the price fields. Split city and region in areaServed.
Trade-off
Needs a business fact from Gareth (is there a real address, and real starting prices). Until then, the safe move is to stop asserting fake specifics rather than assert wrong ones.

Strategic, from the brief direction, not this week's tickets

S1. Treat AI Overview capture as a design goal, not an afterthought

STRATEGIC
The signal and the move
Google now calls GEO "still SEO," and citations come from pages that rank organically and answer the query directly with clean entity markup. Everything needed for this is already in the fix list: be discoverable (C1, C2), answer first (H3), and mark up entities cleanly (M2, M3). There is no separate AI project to build and, per the brief, no llms.txt or AI specific schema to add. The strategic point is simply to make "does the opening answer the question" a standing item in the Auditor gate for every future batch.

S2. Build the agent content-refresh workflow the brief flagged

STRATEGIC
The signal and the move
Ahrefs demonstrated an agent that finds pages with stale data and refreshes them. This site's pages carry pricing, seasonality, and service claims that go out of date, and the site already has a worker based production model. A quarterly refresh pass, driven from the structured pestProfile and costNote fields, would keep freshness signals current and fits the existing pipeline. Worth speccing as its own future block, not part of this remediation.

S3. Operational notes from the brief

STRATEGIC
Watch, do not act
The Google Search Console page indexing report has been stale since 11 June, so for the next few weeks do not judge whether the newly discoverable city pages are indexed by that bulk report. Use the URL Inspection tool for spot checks instead. The Open Knowledge Format idea from Marie Haynes is genuinely interesting but is early and unproven, so it stays a watch item, not a build. Do not chase paid brand mentions; the brief confirms they do not work on Google's current system and are a flagged black hat tactic.

Recommended sequence

  1. Block 1, discoverability (Sonnet). C1 sitemap and C2 state to city links together, plus H2 broken nearby links and M5 canonicals. This is the highest impact and lowest risk work, and it should ship first as one block. After this, the money pages can actually be found.
  2. Block 2, trust and schema (Sonnet, with one decision from Gareth). H1 fake ratings (pending Gareth's call), M1 broken assets, M2 breadcrumbs, M3 entity glue, M6 dead links, M7 NAP and offers, plus the L items. Centralize schema in one helper as you go.
  3. Block 3, answer first content (Opus designs, Sonnet executes). H3 intro rewrites and M4 heading rotation. Opus writes the rubric and checks a sample, then Sonnet runs the bulk against the Auditor gate. This is the AI Overview play and the one place where careless execution could create new duplication, so it goes last and behind a quality gate.

Blocks 1 and 2 are safe for a Sonnet only session working from the build plan. Block 3 needs Opus at the design and sampling stage before Sonnet touches 2,151 pages.

What is already good, and should not be touched. City content is genuinely per location, not find and replace, and the per pest notes and FAQs are strong and specific. Pages are fully static server rendered with no images or heavy JavaScript on the city templates, fonts load without blocking, and the domain is consistent https non www. Canonical coverage exists on every route. The absence of llms.txt and AI specific schema is correct, not a gap. The problems are discoverability plumbing and schema trust, not the underlying content, which is the encouraging part: fix the plumbing and a lot of real content becomes visible at once.
